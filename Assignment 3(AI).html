#Which metric is most important for your business problem?
#Ans: Early repayment means the bank loses future interest income. Missing someone who will repay early (a False Negative) is more costly than a false alarm — the bank cannot hedge its rate risk or redeploy capital in time

#What do false positives and false negatives mean in your dataset?
#Ans: Model predicts a borrower will repay early — but they don't. Bank prepares unnecessarily: may offer retention deals or restructure the loan for no reason. Low-cost mistake.
#Model misses a real early repayer. Bank is caught off-guard: loses what they expected interest, misses the chance to renegotiate or redeploy capital. High-cost mistake.

#What is one possible limitation or bias in your model?
#Ans:Only 69 records were used for testing, and early repayers (Yes) are the minority class. The model sees far fewer "Yes" examples to learn from, which likely suppresses Recall. Results may not hold on a larger or more diverse loan portfolio. Models trained mainly on one customer profile may also perform differently across income levels, loan types, or employment sectors.

#Why should human judgment still be used? answer these questions on my data
#Ans: Even the better model misclassifies roughly 1 in 4 cases. Loan decisions carry real financial and ethical consequences like, a wrong call can mean a customer loses a fair renegotiation opportunity, or the bank takes an avoidable loss. Models also cannot account for context the data doesn't capture: a borrower who received an inheritance, changed jobs, or is going through a life event. Human loan officers can weigh these factors, question edge cases, and catch patterns the model was never trained to see. The model is a decision-support tool, not a replacement for judgment.
